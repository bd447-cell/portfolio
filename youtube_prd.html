YouTube Collections: A Better Way to Save and Organize Videos
A product requirements document for a first-party folder-based video organization system — addressing one of YouTube's most persistently requested and most neglected user needs.
By Bhavya Diwakar
Product YouTube (Google)
Stage v1 Scoped Proposal
Updated June 2026
TL;DR
The Problem
YouTube's Watch Later queue is a flat, unsortable list — users who save frequently can't find or act on saved videos, so the feature creates anxiety instead of reducing it.
The Solution
YouTube Collections — a lightweight folder-based system letting users create named collections, move saved videos into them, and filter by completion status.
Why Now
YouTube Shorts' rise splits user intent at the point of saving. The platform now serves two incompatible viewing modes that one flat list cannot serve well.
Problem Statement
YouTube's Watch Later playlist is the platform's primary saving mechanism — but it scales terribly. Once a user has saved more than ~30 videos, the list becomes effectively unusable: there's no way to group by topic, filter by watched/unwatched, or distinguish between "watch tonight" and "reference later." The result is a queue that grows faster than it shrinks, producing a cognitive backlog users eventually abandon entirely.
This isn't a niche complaint. It's one of the most upvoted feature requests in YouTube's feedback forums and has been surfaced consistently on Reddit for over five years. Watch Later exists specifically to reduce friction around intent-to-watch — but at scale it creates more friction than it resolves.
The core failure: Watch Later was designed for one use case (save a video to watch tonight) but users have adopted it for four distinct jobs: saving tutorials, queuing entertainment, bookmarking reference content, and archiving videos to share. One flat list cannot serve four different jobs.

User Persona
Personas defined by behavior, not demographics.
The Intentional Saver
Primary · The user this feature is built for
Uses YouTube as a learning and reference tool. Saves videos with explicit intent — tutorials to follow, talks to revisit, videos to share. Saves 5–20 videos/week across multiple topic areas. Maintains private playlists as a workaround, but the playlist UX is too heavy for quick-saving behavior.
Job to be Done
When I find a valuable video I can't watch now, I want to save it to a meaningful place so I can reliably find and act on it later — without wading through unrelated content.
Current Workaround
Maintains 4–8 manual private playlists ("coding," "design," "watch this weekend") — but the save flow doesn't support this, so they abandon the queue or spend time manually reorganizing.
The Casual Queue-Builder
Secondary · Benefits from this feature but is not the primary design target
Uses Watch Later as a simple "not now" queue. Saves entertainment videos, watches them the same week, repeats. Queue stays manageable. Benefits from completion filtering but doesn't need multi-folder organization. The core organizing logic must not add friction to their simple use case.
Success Metrics
Defined before the solution — these constrain what we build, not just measure it.
★ North Star
North Star
Watch Later completion rate at 30 days. % of saved videos a user watches within 30 days of saving. Target: +15% within 6 months of launch among users with 30+ saved items. Directly measures whether the feature resolves the graveyard problem.
Secondary
Secondary
Collection creation rate. % of eligible users (≥15 saved videos) who create at least one Collection within 30 days of feature availability.
Secondary
Save-to-collection rate. % of save actions that add a video directly to a named Collection (vs. defaulting to Watch Later). Increasing this over time is the long-term goal.
Secondary
Cross-session return rate to Collections. % of users who open a Collection in a session starting from Library or Home — indicating Collections are becoming a real entry point, not a buried feature.
⚠ Guardrail
Guardrail
Save completion rate must not drop. Current Watch Later save flow is one tap. If Collection assignment adds friction that causes users to abandon the save, we've made the product worse. Must not decline by more than 2% vs. control.
Guardrail
Watch time on saved content must not decline. If Collections cause users to over-organize rather than watch, we've built a procrastination tool. Watch time accessed through Library must be flat or positive vs. pre-launch.

Why this scope: v1 solves the core organization problem without requiring AI infrastructure or social graph work. Shared Collections triple build time. Auto-categorization is v2 only — we need real usage data on how users self-organize before we try to predict it.
The Solution
YouTube Collections is a lightweight folder-based organization layer sitting between Watch Later (quick save) and Playlists (curated, potentially public). Designed to match the mental model users already have — they think in topics — without playlist management overhead.
01Collections in Library
A "Collections" row appears in the Library tab, above Watch Later. Each Collection shows a thumbnail grid, video count, and last-added timestamp. Creating one is one tap — name it, done. No description, no cover photo, no playlist overhead. The mental model is a folder, not a playlist.
02Save flow with Collection assignment
When a user taps "Save," the bottom sheet shows Watch Later (default, unchanged) plus their top 3 Collections as quick-add options and a "New Collection" option. One additional tap assigns a video. If ignored, behavior is identical to today — zero added friction for the casual saver.
03Watch Later completion filter
A Watched / Unwatched toggle at the top of Watch Later, scoped to videos YouTube knows have been completed (≥80% viewed). No ML required. This alone significantly reduces the perceived backlog for long-time users and gives them a clear action: clear your watched videos.
04Bulk move from Watch Later
An "Organize" mode lets users multi-select videos and move them to a Collection in one action. This is the one-time onboarding moment that makes the feature immediately valuable to existing heavy users — and gives us behavioral data to inform v2 auto-categorization.
Edge Cases & Open Questions
?
What happens to existing Watch Later when Collections launch? No migration. Watch Later remains as-is. Collections is additive, not a replacement. Forcing migration creates confusion and support load.
?
Can a video be in both Watch Later and a Collection? Yes — separate states. Watch Later = "save for later." Collections = "file in topic." A video can exist in both without duplication on the watch side.
?
What if a video is deleted by the creator after being saved? Same behavior as Watch Later today — shows as unavailable. Existing known state; no need to solve in v1.
?
How does this interact with Premium offline downloads? Collections do not support offline download in v1 — v2 dependency on the Premium team's roadmap. Needs a decision before launch to avoid user confusion.
?
20 Collection limit — right number? Chosen to prevent storage and UI sprawl, not based on research. Validate through beta: if >10% of heavy users hit the cap in the first 90 days, it's too low.
Key Decision Made
Collections are private-only in v1. Sharing infrastructure doubles scope and introduces content moderation concerns. The core user problem is personal organization, not sharing. Ship private-first, validate retention, revisit sharing in v2.
Key Decision Made
No AI auto-categorization in v1. No ground truth for how users want to categorize their saves. Building AI suggestions before real Collection data is building on sand. Ship the manual tool first, mine usage patterns, then train on it.
The one-sentence PM rationale
YouTube has already earned the save — it just hasn't built a place to put it; Collections turns a feature that produces anxiety into one that produces trust, without touching the recommendation engine, the monetization model, or anything that matters to the platform's revenue logic.
